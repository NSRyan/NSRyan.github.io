2. Licensing your work

I am in no way a lawyer, but I would encourage you to think about licensing as something beyond the legal stuff that comes with it. Licensing tells people how they can interact with your work, and, especially in a world where you can see most of what I’m working on at any given time, having a simple license in place is a great first step to letting people know how I want them to attribute me. Beyond attribution, a license tells people information about your own responsibilities to them as users. Much of the scientific codes I have worked with are provided as-is, but the license is how you can establish the relationship between users and developers. One of the themes that I have and will continue to hit on in open science is sharing justification and thought behind your decisions.

This whole blog is about open science, but I recognize that not every field or every project makes sense to exist out in the open in their entireties. These projects can also benefit from licensing, even if they are private repositories on Github or exist only in your handwritten lab notebook. In such projects, a license governs how collaborators extend or make public versions of your original work (you’ll see this referred to as copyleft or viral licensing, where derivatives or products are governed by the same licenses as the original work).
